# Bloom Boys Opportunity Scoring Requirements

**Status:** Draft v1  
**Applies to:** Bloom Boys CRM  
**Primary purpose:** Rank which opportunities Alex and Sam should pursue first  
**Default active cycle:** 2027

---

## 1. Purpose

The opportunity score must help Bloom Boys answer one practical question:

> Which opportunity should Alex or Sam work on next?

The score must balance potential value with the realistic likelihood that Bloom Boys can actually obtain approval and operate at the event.

The CRM must use **one overall opportunity score out of 100**.

It must not display separate value, readiness and priority scores as competing headline numbers.

However, the one score must remain explainable. The CRM should show the category breakdown behind the total so Alex and Sam can understand why an opportunity ranked highly or poorly.

---

## 2. Core Scoring Principle

The score must favour opportunities that are both:

- Valuable enough to matter
- Realistically approvable

Approval likelihood is more important than raw event size.

A smaller school with a reachable principal, clear approval route and genuine interest may rank above a much larger school where:

- No operational contact is known
- The venue is unknown
- The division approval process is unclear
- An exclusive vendor may already exist
- The information is outdated

The score should not reward size alone.

---

## 3. One Overall Score

Every opportunity receives one score from 0 to 100.

The score is calculated from ten visible categories:

| Category | Maximum points |
|---|---:|
| Likelihood of approval | 20 |
| Engagement and progress | 15 |
| Accessibility of decision-makers | 12 |
| Strategic value and organization unlock | 10 |
| Product fit | 10 |
| Event size and attendance | 10 |
| Timing and information quality | 8 |
| Venue feasibility | 5 |
| Competition risk | 5 |
| Repeat annual value and travel feasibility | 5 |
| **Total** | **100** |

The CRM must show:

- Overall score
- Category scores
- Short explanation for each category
- Main blockers
- Main reasons the opportunity ranks highly
- Date the score was last recalculated
- Whether the score uses confirmed, estimated or missing information

---

## 4. Scoring Must Be Dynamic

The score is not permanent.

It should change when meaningful information changes.

Examples:

- A principal replies
- A correct graduation coordinator is identified
- Verbal interest is received
- The venue is confirmed
- Division approval is required
- A proposal is requested
- An exclusive competitor is discovered
- A 2027 date is confirmed
- The event passes
- Bloom Boys receives written approval
- The opportunity is moved to a later year

The system must preserve score history so Alex and Sam can see how and why the ranking changed.

Each recalculation should record:

- Previous score
- New score
- Date and time
- User or system action that caused the change
- Changed factors
- Optional note

---

## 5. Likelihood of Approval — 20 Points

This is the most important category.

The score should reflect how realistically Bloom Boys can obtain the permissions required to operate.

### Suggested scoring

| Situation | Points |
|---|---:|
| Written approval obtained from all known required authorities | 20 |
| Verbal approval from the main authority and remaining steps appear routine | 17–19 |
| Strong interest and the approval route is clearly understood | 14–16 |
| Approval seems possible but one major authority is still uncertain | 10–13 |
| Multiple approvals are required and the process is unclear | 6–9 |
| Major policy, procurement or venue barrier exists | 2–5 |
| Approval appears impossible or has been explicitly denied | 0–1 |

Approval authorities may include:

- School
- School division
- Institution
- Faculty or department
- Venue
- Procurement office
- Fundraising authority
- Branding authority
- Insurance or risk office
- Community or Indigenous education authority

The CRM must not treat school interest as complete approval when division or venue approval may still be required.

The score explanation should identify the current approval ladder.

Example:

```text
School interest: verbal
Division approval: unknown
Venue approval: not started
Procurement review: likely not required
```

---

## 6. Engagement and Progress — 15 Points

This category measures actual movement.

### Suggested scoring

| Situation | Points |
|---|---:|
| Proposal requested, meeting completed or written next step agreed | 13–15 |
| Verbal interest received | 11–13 |
| Positive response or correct decision-maker engaged | 8–10 |
| Initial contact sent and awaiting response | 4–6 |
| Correct contact identified but not yet contacted | 3–4 |
| No outreach yet | 1–2 |
| Repeated no response or negative response | 0–2 |

Important rules:

- Verbal interest must create a meaningful score increase.
- A meeting scheduled or proposal requested should create a larger increase.
- This category should not override a confirmed legal, procurement or exclusivity barrier.
- A declined opportunity should normally receive little or no engagement credit for the active year.
- “Open to next year” should move the opportunity into the next cycle rather than artificially keeping the current-year score high.

---

## 7. Accessibility of Decision-Makers — 12 Points

This category measures how easily Bloom Boys can reach the correct person.

### Suggested scoring

| Situation | Points |
|---|---:|
| Named operational decision-maker with verified direct email and phone | 11–12 |
| Named operational contact with one reliable channel | 9–10 |
| Strong departmental or school-office routing contact | 7–8 |
| General organization contact with a usable phone route | 5–6 |
| Only senior influence contacts or trustees found | 2–4 |
| No reliable contact route | 0–1 |

Operational contacts should rank above symbolic or influence contacts.

Examples of stronger contacts:

- Graduation coordinator
- Principal
- Vice-principal responsible for graduation
- Administrative assistant who can route accurately
- Venue event manager
- Procurement or operations contact
- Convocation office
- Student organization executive responsible for the event

Trustees should not increase this category unless there is official evidence that they control the event.

---

## 8. Strategic Value and Organization Unlock — 10 Points

This category measures whether one opportunity could unlock several others.

Examples:

- GSCS division approval could unlock several Catholic high schools
- A venue relationship could unlock several school graduations
- A university convocation office could control several ceremonies
- A professional body may run the same event annually
- A student union relationship may create introductions across faculties or clubs

### Suggested scoring

| Strategic effect | Points |
|---|---:|
| Could realistically unlock 6 or more strong child opportunities | 9–10 |
| Could unlock 3–5 meaningful opportunities | 7–8 |
| Could unlock 2 related opportunities | 5–6 |
| Valuable mainly as one standalone event | 3–4 |
| Little wider strategic value | 0–2 |

Rules:

- School divisions and umbrella organizations may receive strategic points.
- Individual schools must still be scored separately.
- A division should not receive strategic points merely because it is large.
- The child opportunities must be realistic and relevant to Bloom Boys.
- Strategic value must not replace the need for actual approval.

---

## 9. Product Fit — 10 Points

The CRM should calculate one general product-fit score while preserving product-specific tags.

### Product tags

- Flowers
- Teddy bears
- Kuki beads
- Necklaces
- Frames
- T-shirts
- School-branded apparel
- School-branded gifts
- Preorder bundles
- Ceremony keepsakes
- Other approved products

### Suggested scoring

| Product fit | Points |
|---|---:|
| Several Bloom Boys products strongly match the audience and ceremony | 9–10 |
| Flowers plus multiple keepsake options appear suitable | 7–8 |
| One or two products appear suitable | 5–6 |
| Limited product match or uncertain buyer interest | 2–4 |
| Products conflict with policy, culture or event format | 0–1 |

Catholic schools must not receive extra points simply for being Catholic.

They may score well when there is real evidence of:

- Existing relationships
- A suitable partnership structure
- Parish or community fit
- Division-wide opportunity
- Strong ceremony demand

---

## 10. Event Size and Attendance — 10 Points

Size matters, but it must not outweigh approval likelihood.

The CRM should use the best available information in this order:

1. Confirmed graduate count
2. Official enrolment-derived estimate
3. Credible public estimate
4. Internal estimate
5. Unknown

### Suggested scoring

| Approximate opportunity size | Points |
|---|---:|
| Very large ceremony or multiple ceremonies with major family attendance | 9–10 |
| Large graduating class and strong family attendance | 7–8 |
| Medium-sized ceremony | 5–6 |
| Small ceremony with viable product demand | 3–4 |
| Very small or uncertain audience | 1–2 |
| No meaningful audience opportunity | 0 |

Estimated counts must be visibly labelled as estimates.

Unknown size should reduce confidence, but it should not automatically make an otherwise strategic opportunity worthless.

---

## 11. Timing and Information Quality — 8 Points

This category combines active-cycle timing with data quality.

The CRM is initially focused on 2027.

### Suggested scoring

| Timing and information status | Points |
|---|---:|
| Confirmed 2027 event, current contacts and ideal outreach timing | 8 |
| 2027 event strongly expected with recent reliable information | 6–7 |
| Annual event is known but date or contact still needs confirmation | 4–5 |
| Important information is estimated, historical or partly outdated | 2–3 |
| Event already passed or information is unreliable | 0–1 |

Recommended 2027 timing treatment:

- September–November 2026: strong relationship-building period
- January–February 2027: strongest proposal and approval period
- March 2027: urgent remaining approval period
- April 2027 onward: difficult unless interest or approval already exists
- After the event: remove from active-cycle priority and create a future revisit record

The CRM should warn when:

- A date is historical
- A copied annual record still contains last year’s details
- The event is approaching without sufficient approval
- The information has not been verified recently

---

## 12. Venue Feasibility — 5 Points

Venue approval can be as important as school or organization approval.

### Suggested scoring

| Venue situation | Points |
|---|---:|
| Venue confirmed, Bloom Boys has prior familiarity and vendor path is clear | 5 |
| Venue confirmed and event contact is accessible | 4 |
| Venue likely workable but written vendor rules are missing | 3 |
| Venue unknown or requires significant research | 1–2 |
| Venue policy appears to block the opportunity | 0 |

Prior familiarity with a venue should improve the score.

However:

- Prior venue experience is not automatic approval
- Approval must be confirmed for the specific event
- Exclusive concessions, retail rules and insurance may differ by client or event

---

## 13. Competition Risk — 5 Points

This category is scored positively when competition barriers are low.

### Suggested scoring

| Competition situation | Points |
|---|---:|
| No existing competitor found and no exclusivity concern identified | 5 |
| Competitor unknown but no warning signs | 4 |
| Competitor suspected or partial product overlap exists | 2–3 |
| Existing vendor confirmed, but non-competing products may still be possible | 2 |
| Strong existing vendor relationship | 1 |
| Exclusive contract blocks Bloom Boys | 0 |

Rules:

- “No competitor found” does not mean no competitor exists.
- Unknown competition should not receive the same confidence as confirmed open access.
- Product adjustments may reduce the penalty.
- An existing flower vendor may still leave room for beads, apparel, frames or other approved products.

---

## 14. Repeat Annual Value and Travel Feasibility — 5 Points

This category combines repeat value with practical travel.

### Annual value

Recurring annual ceremonies should score above one-time events when all other factors are equal.

### Travel rules

The following major Saskatchewan markets should be treated as broadly equal for travel scoring:

- Saskatoon
- Regina
- Prince Albert
- Moose Jaw
- North Battleford
- Yorkton
- Swift Current
- Lloydminster
- Other major regional centres with strong attendance

Warman and Martensville should also be treated as highly practical.

Distance alone should not significantly reduce the score of a major event.

A travel penalty should mainly apply when:

- The event is small
- The location is remote
- The opportunity requires substantial travel or accommodation
- Several operational trips would be required
- Expected demand does not justify the travel burden

### Suggested scoring

| Annual and travel value | Points |
|---|---:|
| Strong annual repeat value and practical market | 5 |
| Good repeat potential or strong major-market event | 4 |
| Moderate annual value with manageable travel | 3 |
| Small or remote event with limited repeat benefit | 1–2 |
| One-time event with poor travel feasibility | 0 |

---

## 15. Tier Definitions

The overall score determines the opportunity tier.

### Tier 1 — 80 to 100

High-value, realistically approvable and appropriate for active 2027 outreach.

Typical characteristics:

- Strong operational contact
- Good likelihood of approval
- Major or strategic event
- Suitable products
- Current information
- Clear next step

### Tier 2 — 65 to 79

Strong opportunity requiring relationship-building, additional approval or research.

Typical characteristics:

- Meaningful value
- One or more unresolved approval layers
- Contact route exists
- Worth pursuing, but not as immediate as Tier 1

### Tier 3 — 45 to 64

Smaller pilot, regional test or incomplete opportunity.

Typical characteristics:

- Moderate product or attendance potential
- Missing data
- Limited access
- Smaller audience
- More difficult travel relative to value

### Tier 4 — 0 to 44

Poor current fit, significant barriers, outdated timing or insufficient practical value.

Typical characteristics:

- Event passed
- Approval unlikely
- Exclusive competitor
- No useful contact route
- Very limited demand
- Major unresolved barriers

Tier thresholds should be configurable by an administrator later, but these must be the initial defaults.

---

## 16. Score Caps and Hard Blockers

Some conditions should cap the score even if other categories are strong.

### Suggested caps

| Condition | Maximum score |
|---|---:|
| Event already passed for the active cycle | 40 |
| Confirmed exclusive contract preventing Bloom Boys participation | 30 |
| Explicit rejection for the active cycle | 35 |
| No reliable organization or event evidence | 50 |
| No possible contact channel | 55 |
| Major unresolved cultural or community permission issue | 55 |
| Venue policy appears to prohibit commercial activity | 45 |

A cap must be visible in the score explanation.

Example:

> Raw score: 76  
> Final score: 45  
> Reason: venue policy currently appears to prohibit outside commercial activity.

The CRM must not hide the uncapped category calculation, because it may become useful if the blocker is resolved.

---

## 17. Engagement Boosts Must Be Included Inside the Score

The CRM should not add mysterious bonus points after calculating the total.

Instead, real engagement must update the **Engagement and Progress** category.

Examples:

- Correct contact identified
- Response received
- Verbal interest
- Introductory meeting
- Proposal requested
- Proposal sent
- Written approval

This keeps the score understandable and prevents totals from exceeding 100.

---

## 18. Missing Information

Missing information should mainly reduce:

- Accessibility of decision-makers
- Timing and information quality
- Venue feasibility
- Approval likelihood

It should not automatically destroy:

- Strategic value
- Event size potential
- Product fit
- Repeat annual value

The CRM must distinguish:

- Confirmed
- Estimated
- Inferred
- Unknown
- Historical
- Outdated
- Conflicting

Every score based on estimated or missing information should carry a confidence warning.

---

## 19. Score Confidence

The CRM should display one confidence label beside the overall score.

Confidence is not a second score.

### Confidence levels

- High
- Medium
- Low

### Suggested rules

**High confidence**

- Most major factors are confirmed
- Current operational contacts are known
- Current event and venue information is sourced
- Approval route is understood

**Medium confidence**

- Some estimates remain
- Event is recurring but the current date is not confirmed
- One approval layer is uncertain
- Contact route exists

**Low confidence**

- Major information is missing
- Records are historical
- Only general or influence contacts exist
- Venue and approval route are unknown
- Score relies heavily on estimates

The score should never appear more precise than the underlying research.

---

## 20. Original Research Scores

The CRM must preserve the original Phase 1 and Phase 2 scores and tiers.

Each imported opportunity should retain:

- Original research phase
- Original source file
- Original score
- Original tier
- Original scoring notes
- Original row ID
- Date imported

The standardized CRM score must be stored separately.

The original score must never be overwritten.

This allows Alex and Sam to compare:

- What the research spreadsheet originally said
- What the live CRM now says
- Why the ranking changed

---

## 21. Division and Child-School Scoring

A division-wide opportunity and its child-school opportunities must be scored independently.

Example:

```text
GSCS Division-Wide Partnership 2027 — score 84
Holy Cross Graduation 2027 — score 91
St. Joseph Graduation 2027 — score 88
Bethlehem Graduation 2027 — score 82
```

Rules:

- School interest may improve the strategic value and approval likelihood of the division opportunity.
- Division approval may improve the approval likelihood of child-school opportunities.
- Scores should not automatically copy between parent and child records.
- Each record must show which connected records influenced its score.
- A division with many schools should only receive strategic points when those schools are realistic Bloom Boys targets.

---

## 22. Venue Relationship Scoring

A venue opportunity may be scored separately from the events held there.

Example:

```text
TCU Place Venue Relationship — score 86
Oskāyak Graduation 2027 — score 78
Another TCU Place Graduation — score 81
```

A strong venue relationship may improve venue-feasibility points for connected events.

It must not create automatic school, division or event approval.

---

## 23. Product-Specific Fit

The headline CRM uses one overall opportunity score.

However, each opportunity should also support product-fit tags and a simple product recommendation.

Example:

```text
Overall product fit: Strong
Flowers: Very strong
Teddy bears: Strong
Kuki beads: Moderate
Frames: Strong
T-shirts: Approval required
```

These product assessments do not need separate numerical scores in version one.

They should help Alex and Sam decide what to propose.

---

## 24. Scoring Workflow

### During import

The system should:

1. Preserve the original Phase 1 or Phase 2 score.
2. Map available fields into the standardized scoring categories.
3. Mark uncertain category values as estimated.
4. Calculate an initial standardized score.
5. Assign a confidence level.
6. List the missing information that would most affect the score.

### During research

The score should update when:

- A contact is confirmed
- A date is confirmed
- A venue is confirmed
- The graduate count is improved
- A policy is found
- Competition is identified
- The approval route is clarified

### During outreach

The score should update when:

- Contact is sent
- A reply arrives
- Verbal interest is received
- A meeting is scheduled
- A proposal is requested
- Approval is granted or denied

---

## 25. Score Explanation

Every opportunity page must include a readable explanation.

Example:

```text
Opportunity Score: 87 — Tier 1
Confidence: Medium

Why it ranks highly:
• Strong likelihood of school approval
• Principal identified and reachable
• Large annual graduating class
• Could support several Bloom Boys products
• Major Saskatchewan market
• Strong repeat annual value

Main blockers:
• Division approval not yet confirmed
• Venue retail rules still need written confirmation
• 2027 ceremony date is estimated
```

The CRM must not display only a number.

---

## 26. Manual Override

Alex and Sam should be able to manually override the calculated score.

Requirements:

- Calculated score remains visible
- Manual score remains visible
- Override reason is required
- User and date are recorded
- Override can be removed
- Score history is preserved

Example:

```text
Calculated score: 72
Manual score: 82
Reason: Existing personal relationship and invitation to submit a proposal
Overridden by: Alex
```

Manual overrides should be exceptional, not the default workflow.

---

## 27. Recalculation Rules

The system should recalculate automatically when a scoring input changes.

It should not recalculate merely because:

- A note was edited
- A file was attached
- A non-scoring tag changed
- A user opened the record

The CRM should provide:

- Recalculate score
- View score breakdown
- View score history
- Restore calculated score
- Compare original research score

---

## 28. Saved Priority Views

The CRM should use the one overall score to support saved views such as:

- Highest-scoring uncontacted opportunities
- Tier 1 opportunities
- Tier 2 opportunities
- High score with low confidence
- Verbal interest
- Proposal requested
- Awaiting division approval
- Awaiting venue approval
- Large events with poor contact access
- Strategic division opportunities
- Major venue opportunities
- 2027 opportunities
- Assigned to Alex
- Assigned to Sam
- Unassigned

The default priority list should sort by:

1. Overall opportunity score
2. Follow-up due date
3. Pipeline progress
4. Upcoming event timing

Users must still be able to change the sorting.

---

## 29. What Must Not Affect the Score Automatically

The following must not automatically increase the score:

- Catholic identity by itself
- A famous organization name
- A trustee contact without operational relevance
- An estimated revenue number
- A large social-media following
- An AI-generated assumption
- A historical event that has not been confirmed for 2027
- The number of contacts when none are operationally useful
- A school logo or strong brand identity by itself

The following must not automatically decrease the score:

- Regina, Prince Albert, Moose Jaw or another major regional city being outside Saskatoon
- Missing data that only affects confidence rather than real opportunity value
- The existence of a competitor when non-competing products may still be allowed
- A division-level approval requirement when the route is clear and realistic

---

## 30. Initial Score Interpretation

The score should be used as a decision-support tool, not as an automatic command.

A high score means:

- Worth serious attention
- Realistic enough to pursue
- Strong enough to justify time
- Clear enough to identify a next action

A low score means:

- Poor fit
- Major barriers
- Weak timing
- Insufficient evidence
- Or limited value compared with stronger opportunities

Alex and Sam may still pursue a lower-scoring opportunity for strategic, relationship or learning reasons.

---

## 31. Acceptance Criteria

The opportunity-scoring system is acceptable only if:

1. Every opportunity has one overall score out of 100.
2. The score is broken into visible categories.
3. Approval likelihood carries more weight than event size.
4. Regina, Prince Albert, Moose Jaw and other major regional cities are not automatically penalized relative to Saskatoon.
5. Catholic schools receive no automatic scoring bonus.
6. Division-wide opportunities can receive strategic unlock points.
7. Individual schools remain separately scored.
8. Venue relationships can improve venue feasibility without creating automatic event approval.
9. Verbal interest creates a meaningful score increase.
10. Proposal requests and meetings create additional progress points.
11. Missing data reduces the relevant categories rather than destroying the whole score.
12. Historical dates are not treated as current.
13. Competition risk is visible and product-specific flexibility can reduce the penalty.
14. Original Phase 1 and Phase 2 scores are preserved.
15. The standardized CRM score is stored separately.
16. Score confidence is shown as High, Medium or Low.
17. Score caps are applied for hard blockers.
18. Manual overrides require a reason and audit record.
19. Score history is preserved.
20. Every score includes a plain-language explanation.
21. The system does not use expected revenue in the initial score.
22. The score can be recalculated when research or outreach changes.
23. The total never exceeds 100.
24. The score helps identify the next best opportunity without replacing human judgment.
